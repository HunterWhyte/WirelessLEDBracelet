Problem Description

Live music events and festivals are a multi-billion dollar industry, and today's fans expect a compelling visual experience along with the music. One technique that has arisen is the use of LED bracelets, worn by attendees, to create light shows in stadiums and at festivals. There are multiple existing solutions for wirelessly-controllable LED bracelets on the market today - these are all single-use devices that are discarded after the event. We aim to provide a higher quality, durable bracelet that can be reused and generate less e-waste than competitors.

The goal is to enhance the experience at live events by connecting attendees to the venue and enabling fan interaction with a sustainable and reusable device that people can take home after the event.

Our controlled project's scope includes having a working prototype LED bracelet that is rechargeable and can be controlled in three different ways:

  1. Wirelessly by a venue to create interesting lighting effects during events.
  2. Wirelessly by a mobile device (via a mobile app) that the bracelet is capable of pairing to for use outside of a concert venue.
  3. By the buttons on the bracelet to toggle the bracelet’s lights on and off, and to iterate through the pre-programmed light patterns.

Features requested by the client that are outside of our project’s scope include:

  • Searching for concerts and purchasing tickets via the mobile app
  • Contactless payment with concessions at a venue

Analysis and Justification of Requirements

Electrical and LED Requirements

1.1 The bracelet must have 3 onboard tri-colour LEDs.

In our discussions with the client, the specification of 3 to 5 groups of LEDs, depending on cost and brightness, was brought up as a requirement. RGB capabilities were also a requirement outlined by the client, thus tri-colour LEDs are required as opposed to single colour.

1.2 The total lumen output of the bracelet must be a minimum of 28 lumens.

The recommended lumen level for LEDs, in areas where overlighting can cause harm to the eyes, ranges from 12 to 100 lumens [1]. Anything lower than the minimum would not emit enough light to be noticeable. Following Requirement 1.1, placing three LEDs on the bracelet results in an overall lumen value of 28 per bracelet, falling within the safe range.

(NumberofLEDs = (Lumens Needed) / (Lumens per LED x CU), Coefficient of Utilisation (CU) for LED is approximately 0.77, Lumens per LED is 12, minimum lumen value for NeoPixel SMD 5050) [2]

1.3 The tri-colour LEDs must be capable of updating the brightness level of each colour at a rate of at least 10Hz.

Requirement 2.6 states that the lights on the bracelet must be able to be controlled by the central controller at a rate of 10Hz. Therefore, the physical LEDs on the bracelet must be capable of being updated at that refresh rate.

1.4 The bracelet must be rechargeable.

The client specified that the bracelet must be reusable to differentiate it from existing products on the market.

1.5 The bracelet must have a battery life of at least 3.5 hours while active.

The client specified that the bracelet must be in use for a minimum of one concert. Using Coldplay as a base case, one of the first bands to use LED bracelets, their setlist runs on average around 2 hours. To accommodate any opening acts as well, another 1.5 hours should be estimated - totalling 3.5 hours of concert time. Active is defined as all LEDs at full brightness 75% of the time.

1.6 The bracelet’s external temperature must not exceed 40°C.

The Standard Guide for Heated System Surface Conditions that Produce Contact Burn Injuries outlines that the pain threshold temperature for human skin is 44°C [3]. Lowering this to 40°C ensures the wearer should never be in pain from the temperature of the device.

Wireless Control Requirements

2.1 The bracelet must be able to pair over Bluetooth to Android and iOS devices supporting Bluetooth 4.0.

In the project proposal, the client states that “[The] bracelet needs a Bluetooth receiver so that the LED(s) can be controlled… by a phone through an app.” Bluetooth version 4.0 and greater support Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) which is designed for very low-power operation [6]. Our application will be using BLE only and not Bluetooth Classic, as it is a low-power application without high throughput needs. Therefore, we must be able to pair to all Android and iOS devices that support BLE.

2.2 The bracelet must be able to have its LEDs controlled by a connected Android or iOS phone supporting Bluetooth 4.0.

Refer to Requirement 2.1.

2.3 A central controller must be able to wirelessly control all LED bracelets in sync.

In the project proposal, the client states that “a transmitter needs to be able to control LED bracelets”. The central controller refers to a transmitter that is capable of controlling the LEDs of many bracelets in real-time.

2.4 The central controller must be capable of controlling the tri-colour LEDs with a resolution of RGB565 (32 levels of brightness for red, 64 for green and 32 for blue).

The bracelet must be capable of full-colour lighting effects using its tri-colour LEDs. When displaying an image on a screen with RGB565 (high colour), there can be some visible banding. However, our application is dealing with discrete LEDs spread out across a crowd, so visible banding is not a concern. Through experimentation with RGB LEDs, it was determined that using 65,536 colours is more than enough to achieve full-colour lighting effects.

2.5 The maximum latency between the central controller and lighting effects on a bracelet must be less than 100ms.

The average detectable difference between when a sound is played and the visual feedback corresponding to that sound is approximately 187ms [15]. Since our bracelet will have lighting effects that are synchronised with music, we must stay below a latency of 100ms to avoid noticeable desync.

2.6 The central controller must be capable of updating the LEDs on the bracelet at a rate of 10Hz.

The command rate of existing products with publicly available technical specifications ranges from 5Hz to 35Hz. Requiring 10 Hz was experimentally determined by creating animations of colour transitions at different refresh rates; anything below 10Hz has a noticeable stuttering effect.

2.7 The central controller must be capable of controlling 23,500 bracelets at once.

The largest indoor stadium in North America (Greensboro Coliseum) has a maximum capacity of 23,500. Accommodating this capacity (23,500) will ensure our controller is functional at all stadiums.

2.8 The central controller must be able to control bracelets in groups.

In the project proposal, the client states that “Lights should be able to change separately for bracelets in different sections of the stadium”. A bracelet must be capable of being assigned to an addressable group.

2.9 The central controller must be able to control up to 32 distinct groups of bracelets.

The number of controllable groups for existing products with publicly available technical specifications ranges from 10 to 84 groups. Through looking at the seating arrangements of various stadiums, and viewing videos of existing products, it was determined that using 32 distinct groups is adequate.

2.10 The maximum number of bracelets in a group is the same as the maximum total number of bracelets: 23,500.

All bracelets should be capable of being controlled together - effectively acting as a single group. Therefore, the maximum number of bracelets in a group is the same as the maximum total number of bracelets being controlled.

2.11 The central controller must be capable of controlling bracelets within a 75m radius line of sight, with a packet error rate of less than 20%.

The value of 75 metres was determined by measuring the sizes of the largest stadiums in North America. The largest-capacity indoor stadium in North America (Greensboro Coliseum) has an estimated maximum indoor distance from the centre of approximately 68 metres. Therefore if the outdoor requirement of a 75 metre radius is met, then it is safe to assume that the range of wireless connectivity is adequate for all indoor stadiums as well. Packet error rate is the ratio of the number of messages sent and never received over the total number of messages sent.

2.12 The central controller must be capable of controlling bracelets in indoor and outdoor stadium environments.

As many live events occur in either indoor or outdoor stadiums, the system must function in both indoor and outdoor stadium environments.

2.13 The bracelet must be able to have its group set by “scanning” the bracelet with a smartphone.

In order to efficiently assign bracelets to groups, bracelets should be capable of being “scanned” with a smartphone using Near Field Communication (NFC).

System Alternatives

Overall System Alternatives

Methodology

Multiple methods were used in the generation of our alternative system designs. One method that we heavily utilised was looking at teardowns of existing products in the market. There are many similar products available that make use of a variety of techniques, and we found it useful to critically consider all of these options when generating our own system alternatives.

Our application is intended to be high volume, low cost, low power, small footprint and computationally light - therefore, a microcontroller is preferred over other options such as an FPGA. Achieving Bluetooth connection with a smartphone requires the BLE protocol stack from application to physical layer. The BLE protocol, although an open specification, is complicated and is almost always implemented by microcontroller manufacturers. Implementing the BLE stack is outside the scope of our project, so a SoC with a 2.4GHz radio and built-in BLE stack is required.

As established, having an SoC with BLE support is required to achieve connection to a smartphone over BLE, so a key subsystem of our design is already determined without any reasonable alternatives. The selection of an SoC with a 2.4GHz radio served to constrain and guide the system alternatives that we generated. The key design question for our system alternatives is how the central controller will wirelessly communicate with bracelets.

In order to effectively compare between our overall system alternatives, six main categories of analysis have been selected. Each of these six metrics will be equally weighted in our decision matrix. The score of an alternative for meeting each metric will be measured relative to all other alternatives using a scale of 0 to 3. These scores will then be summed to determine the best overall solution.  

The breakdown for the numerical values used for scoring is as follows:

0 = worst - clear and prohibitive disadvantage.

1 = bad - clear disadvantage.

2 = adequate - no clear advantage.

3 = good - clear advantage.

Three metrics for the performance of the connection between the central controller and bracelets have been chosen. The connectivity metrics that we will be using are:

  1. Bandwidth: What is the approximate data rate that this design is capable of? (Requirements 2.6, 2.7, 2.9)

Required data rate estimation based on requirements:

  • (16b colour data + 16b control data) x 10Hz x 32 groups = ~10kbit/s being transmitted total by the central controller.
  • (16b colour data + 16b control data) x 10Hz = ~320bit/s required by received by each bracelet
  1. Latency: What is the expected latency between central controller and bracelet? (Requirement 2.5)
  • A maximum of 100ms is required.
  1. Reliability: What is the expected reliability of the communication, especially at the maximum required range of 75m?

Estimations and calculations will be utilised where practical, otherwise, qualitative comparisons will be used. The three non-technical requirements that we will be analysing are:

  1. Hardware Cost: What is the approximate cost of materials for this design? (Requirements 6.1, 6.2)
  2. Development cost: What is the expected development cost when compared to alternatives?
  3. Usability, Safety, environmental and social considerations: Are there any major considerations when compared to alternatives?

Alternative 1: Infrared

Figure 1. Infrared system alternative block diagram

1.1 Summary

The central controller subsystem includes an infrared (IR) emitter and the bracelets have an infrared receiver. This method is used by the company PixMob: a successful wireless LED bracelet vendor. Their website states “For wireless object control, we use an infrared signal… It’s fast (20 FPS), affordable, and avoids any interference with existing radio communications”[5]. To achieve the bracelet grouping (Requirement 2.8), the IR emitters are mounted in the centre of a venue above the crowd and rotate in order to direct signals at different parts of the crowd. This technique is described by PixMob in their blog post Up Close: The PixMob Moving Head [5]. In our design, the IR receiver exists as a peripheral to the main BLE SoC, as seen in the block diagram. In our findings, regular consumer IR transmitters and receivers (such as those used commonly in TV remotes) only work reliably within 10m [6]. To achieve the range required by our project, would require IR transmitters that are higher power and more complicated than what is available off the shelf.

1.2 Analysis

1.2.1 Bandwidth (1)

The data rate of a standard 36kHz carrier frequency IR remote is generally in the range of 100-2000 bit/s [6]. The estimated 320bit/s data rate required by each bracelet is within the capability of off the shelf IR transmitter hardware. To meet the data rate required on the transmitter side, an array of multiple IR emitters would be needed. There is a clear disadvantage in bandwidth when compared to other solutions that can meet the required data rate with a single transmitter.

1.2.2 Latency (2)

IR transmissions themselves are instantaneous. However, the technique used by PixMob involves scanning the crowd with directional IR emitters in order to achieve the grouping of bracelets [5]. Having the IR emitters scan across the crowd means that there is some time multiplexing involved and instant transmission to every bracelet is not possible. The latency requirement is attainable for this design but has some risks, so there is no clear advantage in latency over other alternatives.

1.2.3 Reliability (2)

An advantage of using IR is that it is not vulnerable to RF interference like other solutions. A disadvantage of this alternative is that the IR transmitter must have line of sight to a bracelet for it to be able to reliably receive. In a crowded venue with bracelets being worn on wrists, there may be many bracelets out of sight of the transmitter. Using IR outdoors (Requirement 2.12) on a sunny day may also present reliability issues. This solution is immune to RF interference, but there are other reliability risks, therefore there is no clear advantage in reliability over other alternatives.

1.2.4 Hardware Cost (1)

On the bracelet side, an infrared receiver module must be added. In order to estimate the cost of adding an infrared receiver we looked at a TSOP382, which can be found for less than $1 CAD. For the transmitter side, it is harder to estimate the exact cost since researching long range infrared has not led to any clear solutions. We do know that the central controller would require multiple IR emitters on some sort of motorised mount in order to scan the crowd. The required central controller hardware cost puts this design at a significant disadvantage relative to alternatives.

1.2.5 Development Cost (0)

The development cost for integrating an IR receiver on the bracelet is estimated to be worse than average when compared to alternatives. The overall development cost for this design is significantly higher than other designs due to the lack of resources or off the shelf solutions for achieving IR transmissions with a long enough range for the central controller.

1.2.6 Usability, Safety, Environmental and Social Considerations (2)

Without detailed research into high power IR transmission, we have determined that this design may have a slight or negligible advantage in safety and social considerations when compared to RF designs. Other designs have to consider the regulations surrounding RF, especially when using high enough power to transmit the required 75m range.

Alternative 2: WiFi via Smartphone Connection

Figure 2. Wifi via Smartphone Connection block diagram

2.1 Summary

The attendee’s smartphone must be connected to the central controllers web server and to the bracelet over BLE. The smartphone application will forward the commands from the web server to the bracelet over the existing BLE connection. For this design, we are considering the possibility that a venue will already have a WiFi network and hardware that can be reused for our purpose. However, we were unable to survey any event centres to determine if this would be the case. To achieve acceptable latency, it is preferable that all smartphones and the web server are on the same local network, but our analysis considers the use case where phones are connected over the internet (through cellular data or otherwise). The obvious disadvantage of this solution is the requirement for the event attendee to pair their smartphone to their bracelet and connect to the WiFi network which is not required by other designs.

2.2 Analysis

2.2.1 Bandwidth (3)

In practice a WiFi (IEEE 802.11) connection is capable of speeds well over 1Mbit/s, a standard BLE connection is also capable of over 1Mbit/s [12] which is well beyond what will ever be required by our application. However, there is significant overhead to a WiFi connection which must be considered for our application given that up to 23,500 phones must be connected at once. If we are assuming that a venue has a WiFi network that can be reused for our purposes, then the large number of connections is less of a concern. With a full WiFi and BLE connection, this design has a clear advantage in bandwidth over alternatives.

2.2.2 Latency (1)

To estimate latency in the case where a venue already has a WiFi network that can be used by all attendees, we experimentally measured the ping (round-trip time) between a smartphone and a computer connected to the same WiFi router. In this case latency was less than 10ms in all test runs, which is well within the required 100ms. To simulate the use case where an attendee is connected to cellular data, we ran the same test but this time with the smartphone connected to cellular data. With the smartphone connected to cellular data the latency fluctuated, peaking at 60ms in the worst case. Although the worst case is still within our requirement, latency may pose a risk on a busier network. This design has a clear disadvantage in latency relative to alternatives which have a more direct method of delivering messages to the bracelets.

2.2.3 Reliability (1)

The reliability of this alternative is a significant risk. Unlike other alternatives, using WiFi to connect the bracelets to the central controller requires two-way communication. There are more points of failure with a WiFi connection when compared to simpler alternatives. Another concern with this solution is the high density of BLE connections, as every bracelet will need to have a bidirectional connection over BLE to a smartphone. The bidirectionality greatly increases the potential for RF interference, especially since most WiFi networks operate in the same 2.4GHz frequency band. It is difficult to determine if the density of BLE devices required would have a noticeable effect on the reliability, but is considered a significant risk and a clear disadvantage.

2.2.4 Hardware Cost (2)

No additional hardware when compared to other alternatives will be needed on the bracelet. For the central controller, if we are assuming that we will be able to use existing WiFi networks at venues, or rely on attendees to connect over cellular data, then there is also no hardware cost for the central controller. In the best case, this design requires no additional hardware, and in the worst case, WiFi APs may be required, putting this design at no clear advantage when compared to alternatives.

2.2.5 Development cost (2)

The challenge of requiring attendees to pair to the bracelet in a crowded venue where potentially hundreds of other bracelets are advertising over BLE poses a significant risk. Streamlining the process of pairing the bracelet, installing the application, and connecting to the web server for the concert to an acceptable level will require a significant amount of development. The advantage of only requiring the BLE connection on the bracelet slightly offsets the additional development cost, therefore this design has no clear advantage when compared to alternatives.

2.2.6 Usability, Safety, Environmental and Social Considerations (1)

A major downside to this alternative is that it requires all attendees to have smartphones capable of connecting to the bracelet over BLE and to the internet. Connecting a bracelet to the smartphone, downloading the required app, and connecting to the web server adds potentially irritating extra steps to the user experience. These extra steps could heavily discourage use, especially by people who are not technologically savvy. Requiring users to connect their phones to the bracelet is a clear disadvantage.

Alternative 3A: 2.4GHz RF

Figure 3. 2.4GHz RF system alternative block diagram

3.1 Summary

The central controller has a 2.4GHz RF transmitter. Since a 2.4GHz transceiver is already required on the bracelet for the BLE connection, the radio can be used to also listen to the central controller. A broadcast topology is used by the central controller since no data needs to be transmitted back to it from the bracelet. The central controller can be transmit-only with the bracelet acting as receive-only. With a broadcast topology, we do not have to worry about maintaining individual connections with each bracelet. The bracelet would periodically listen using another 2.4GHz protocol, searching for the central controller in between BLE activity. If messages from the central controller are found, the bracelet will start tracking those messages and disable its BLE connection.

3.2 Analysis

3.2.1 Bandwidth (2)

The following estimations use the ANT protocol in a broadcast configuration where the bracelet is receive-only. Each ANT packet contains an 8 byte payload, and the maximum frequency of a transmit-only ANT channel is 200Hz. Using a single transmitter we can achieve 12.8kbit/s and meet the bandwidth requirement. Since ANT has 80 frequency channels, it is also possible to have multiple transmitters broadcast concurrently on different frequencies to further increase the data rate. This design has no clear advantage in bandwidth over alternative designs.

3.2.2 Latency (2)

Transmissions from the central controller to the bracelet are instant. If the transmitter is using ANT and time multiplexing the data, with data for each individual group being sent at a frequency of 10Hz, then if one packet is missed the bracelet will not be updated for another 100ms. The latency in the case of missed packets is shared between all alternatives however, and could be mitigated in this case by running the ANT channel at a higher frequency for redundancy. This design has no clear advantage in latency over other designs.

3.2.3 Reliability (1)

The reliability of using 2.4GHz radio is a significant risk for this design. The 2.4GHz ISM frequency band is used by other popular wireless protocols like Bluetooth and WiFi. The signal is at risk of interference, especially in a crowded concert environment with a large number of people and their devices. This design has a clear disadvantage when compared to alternatives that are less prone to interference.

3.2.4 Hardware Cost (2)

This alternative requires no additional hardware on the bracelet, as it reuses the existing BLE module. For the central controller, a 2.4GHz transmitter will be required, which talks over USB to the desktop application. To estimate the cost of the central controller hardware we looked at a nRF24L01 2.4GHz radio which can be found for less than $5CAD, within our cost requirement but worse than alternative 2 which may require no hardware. This design has no clear advantage in hardware cost over other designs.

3.2.5 Development cost (3)

Reusing the BLE SoC on the bracelet decreases the development cost significantly. There are multiple 2.4GHz protocols to choose from, and using an existing protocol that meets our needs will decrease development cost significantly. There is also a wide selection of BLE capable SoCs that support running another 2.4GHz protocol in coexistence, which can save considerable firmware development time. The existence of open 2.4GHz protocols and the convenience of having only one receiver puts this design at a clear advantage in development cost.

3.2.6 Usability, Safety, Environmental and Social Considerations (2)

This design would enable the bracelet to work in the concert setting without ever having paired to the attendee’s phone. Having the bracelet work standalone removes any restrictions for who is able to participate. Having a 2.4GHz radio transmit the full required range may require relatively high transmission power, and keeping this power within safety regulations may pose a risk for our design. This design has no clear advantage over other designs.

Alternative 3B: Sub-1GHz RF

Figure 4. Sub-1GHz RF system alternative block diagram

4.1 Summary

The central controller subsystem has a sub-1GHz RF transmitter. Instead of reusing the BLE module on the bracelet, a sub-1GHz receiver must be added alongside. Using sub-1GHz radio is the approach that the company Xylobands uses [7]. Xylobands makes use of a Si4362 low-power receiver and uses a proprietary protocol to control their bracelets. In our analysis we will be looking at the Si4362 to estimate values.

4.2 Analysis

4.2.1 Bandwidth (2)

The Si4362 receiver is capable of receiving at a rate of 120 kbit/s which meets our bandwidth requirement. A single transmitter would be able to time multiplex across all the groups and achieve the required data rate. This design is capable of meeting the requirement, but has no clear advantage over alternatives.

4.2.2 Latency (2)

Transmissions from the central controller to the bracelet are instant. If the transmitter is capable of transmitting at the rate stated in the Si4362 datasheet, then it may be possible to transmit at a rate much higher than what is required. Higher rates would thereby reduce the latency effect of missed packets substantially. This design has no clear latency advantage over alternatives.

4.2.3 Reliability (3)

Using sub-1GHz RF is typically more reliable than 2.4GHz RF, as there are less devices using sub-1GHz and thus less interference. In general, sub-1GHz is also capable of a longer range than 2.4GHz. This alternative has a significant advantage in terms of reliability when compared with other designs.

4.2.4 Hardware Cost (1)

To achieve both 2.4GHz and sub-1GHz radio on the bracelet, two SoCs will likely be needed. Dual-band SoCs that support both 2.4GHz and sub-1GHz do exist, but their cost is higher on average than using two separate, single band SoC. As a rough estimate, taking an nRF52832 as the BLE SoC and a Si4362 as the sub-1GHz receiver, the total cost for both modules is approximately $10 CAD. Likewise, looking at a dual-band SoC, CC1350F128RGZT,  the cost is roughly $15 CAD. In the case of Alternative 3A, the cost of a single nRF52832 is approximately $5CAD putting this design at a clear disadvantage.

4.2.5 Development cost (1)

The development cost of adding the sub-1GHz receiver alongside the main SoC is significant. The lack of open standards for use in the sub-1GHz band is also a significant factor. Likely a pre-existing proprietary protocol (limited by selected IC) would need to be used, which can greatly increase our development time. When contrasted with Alternative 3A, which has many open protocols to choose from, this design is at a clear disadvantage.

4.2.6 Usability, Safety, Environmental and Social Considerations (2)

Much like Alternative 3A, this design would enable the bracelet to work in the concert setting without ever having paired to the attendee’s phone. Having the bracelet work standalone removes any restrictions for who is able to participate. There is no clear advantage over alternatives.

Overall Analysis and Selection

A decision matrix was used, summing the values assigned to each alternative for each metric, as discussed in the Methods for Generation and Analysis section. Our analysis concluded that Alternative 3A: 2.4GHz RF had the highest score, and was therefore selected for our system design.

To summarise our main findings, we determined that the infrared alternative had significant development challenges for achieving the data rate and range required, and the development cost was too much of a risk for this design to be practical within our scope. When looking at the WiFi via Smartphone alternative, the usability risk of requiring attendees to pair their smartphone to the bracelet and download the mobile app in order for the bracelet to be usable in a live event setting was too large of a disadvantage. The decision between 2.4GHz and sub-1GHz RF was close and both alternatives are very reasonable designs. Ultimately, the deciding factor was the development and hardware cost of adding a second radio to the bracelet for sub-1GHz, whereas the 2.4GHz solution can reuse the radio being used by the smartphone BLE connection.

Wireless Protocol Alternatives

Summary

Having chosen Alternative 3A: 2.4GHz RF, a protocol must be selected for sending data from the central controller to the bracelets.

Key qualities for selected 2.4GHz protocol (see previous section for estimate calculations):

  • Capable of transmitting an estimated data rate of 10kbit/s.
  • Capable of receiving an estimated data rate of 320bit/s.
  • Latency of less than 100ms.
  • Range of 75m.
  • Low power for receiver.
  • As little transmitter hardware as possible (ideally a single unit)
  • Capable of transmitting to 23,500 bracelets at once.

In order to control the large number of bracelets required, the central controller groups bracelets into 32 individually controllable channels. The data sent to all bracelets within a group is the same. In our design, the bracelet has nothing to send back to the central controller so a broadcast topology without connections is preferred.

Having a broadcast topology enables us to have an asymmetrical configuration where the central controller is transmitting at a high power and long range without needing to worry about receiving back from the bracelets. A unidirectional topology prevents us from using acknowledgement messages/confirmation of receipt, however, not having confirmation is acceptable for our application since the penalty for a missed message is a slightly delayed or missed lighting effect. Additionally, having to maintain an individual connection from 23,500 bracelets at once would require a very high bandwidth. The data being broadcasted by the central controller is not sensitive and requires no encryption.

Many 2.4GHz protocols are intended for IoT applications and use a mesh topology instead of a broadcast topology, so we have considered multiple mesh topologies. A mesh topology is less than ideal because it introduces unnecessary overhead and complexity for our use case where individual bracelets have nothing to transmit and all data will be coming from the single source of the central controller.

Alternatives Analysis

Bluetooth Low Energy

Bluetooth low energy is the protocol that is already being used for the smartphone connection to the bracelet, which makes it an attractive option. The typical BLE use case is a low power, point to point connection, although a one way broadcast topology does exist. BLE transmit-only beacons advertise across the standard BLE advertising channels and are meant for very low data rate applications. Using BLE with beacons would struggle to meet our data rate or latency requirements. BLE mesh is another BLE-based option. In a test conducted by silicon labs with a large network of 192 nodes and a small payload of 5 bytes, Bluetooth mesh latency spread past 250ms, exceeding our latency requirement in some cases [8].

Zigbee

Zigbee is a 2.4GHz mesh based protocol, and has the same disadvantages as Bluetooth mesh. In the same Silicon Labs study outlined previously, the measured max latency was 130ms, exceeding our latency requirement in some cases.

WiFi (IEEE 802.11)

WiFi is capable of transmitting on 2.4GHz, however using WiFi requires an authenticated bidirectional connection which incurs a lot of overhead. Connecting every bracelet over WiFi would add unnecessary complexity for our relatively error tolerant, one-way application.

LoRa

LoRa is intended for very high range, low data rate applications. LoRaWAN is the high-level stack used in IoT applications and is not intended for realtime/low latency applications. Even without the extra overhead of LoRaWAN, the data rate of LoRa may be too low, with too high of latency. Connectionless broadcast is also not supported.

ANT

ANT is an open but proprietary wireless protocol typically intended for low power, low throughput applications. ANT+ is the high level network protocol that defines device profiles for typical use cases, which is mainly for short range consumer fitness trackers and sensors. However, the base ANT protocol (physical and data link layer) is flexible, lightweight, and capable of a variety of topologies.

The maximum throughput of ANT, 12.8kbit/s, is substantially lower than a full point to point BLE connection, but it is still within our required data rate. The standard payload size for an ANT channel is 8 bytes which means we would have to split data for our groups into multiple ANT “channels” which are automatically managed and time-multiplexed by the ANT protocol stack. A single ANT transmitter will be able to handle transmitting to all 32 groups within a latency of less than 100ms, however since we are time-multiplexing the transmission will not be instant to all groups. The main advantage of using ANT is that it is capable of a broadcast topology and can save us the development cost of creating a protocol from scratch starting at the physical layer.

For our application, the ANT transmitter can be configured with multiple channels each transmitting at a rate of 10Hz to all of the bracelets. For a receiver to track a broadcaster, it only has to know the ANT channel configuration of the target transmitter [9].

Some disadvantages to using ANT include that it is closed source so we are restricted to using modules with an ANT stack available. ANT’s physical modulation parameters are also not publicly available so it is difficult to estimate the range we will be able to get out of a transmitter without experimentation. According to Garmin Canada (the developer of ANT) “The range depends on a variety of factors including RF design, enclosure, RF environment, etc. In general ANT modules have been designed to work for 30m line of sight. It is also possible to extend the range by including an external power amplifier, linear noise amplifier, directional antenna, etc.”[13]. Our central transmitter would require an external power amplifier. ANT also requires a per-device licence for commercial use of some modules at a rate of $0.08CAD.

Proprietary Protocol

Although ANT is capable of meeting all of our requirements, using a proprietary protocol may be preferable since there are ANT features  that we will not use (making it slightly overkill for our application).

If we were to design the ideal 2.4GHz protocol for this application, we could use a very simple one-way broadcast that transmits data for all groups in a single packet. To achieve the one-way broadcast we could use a similar tracking method to ANT where a device listens continuously searching for a transmitter on a frequency and then tracks its periodic transmissions using the predefined rate. If we used our own protocol, we may be able to tweak modulation parameters in a way that is favourable for our relatively long range, low data rate application. We could also create a packet format with less overhead, and have a wider selection of devices to choose from when compared to ANT.

Selection

BLE, Zigbee, WiFi and LoRa all require bidirectional communication and are not well suited for our extremely basic application. The two most reasonable options out of the six alternatives analysed are a proprietary protocol and ANT. Both ANT and creating a proprietary protocol are very reasonable choices.

A major factor in our decision is the scope of the project. Implementing the physical layer of a 2.4GHz protocol would be a major risk in our design and could increase the development cost substantially, whereas ANT is a proven technology and will be less of a risk to use. Ultimately, we decided to use ANT due to the low development cost and the ability to use ANT in a simple way that is well suited to our application. Details on our specific usage of ANT are included in the System Design section of this report.

System Design

Summary

A block diagram for the 2.4GHz RF solution is shown in Figure 5. In the block diagram the system is separated by the three physical items, the central controller, the bracelet, and a smartphone, each of these items have subsystems within. In this design, a 2.4GHz SoC is used to connect to a smartphone over BLE as well as receive messages from a central controller over ANT. The SoC also acts as the main processor for the device, reading input from the button and an onboard accelerometer, and controlling the LED drivers. On the connected smartphone there is an app that is capable of configuring the LEDs on the bracelet. The central controller has a desktop application which connects over USB to a 2.4GHz SoC that acts as the transmitter to forward messages using ANT to all the bracelets. In order to achieve grouping as described in requirement 2.8, 2.9, and 2.13, bracelets can be assigned groups using NFC with the smartphone app.

Figure 5. System design block diagram

Functionality

There are three independent sources of control for the bracelet:

  1. Controlled by the physical buttons
  2. Controlled by the smartphone
  3. Controlled by the central controller over ANT

While not in any of the active control modes, the SoC will be in a low power sleep mode, LEDs will be off, and the radio will be disabled. A state diagram including the transitions between the three control modes and sleep mode is shown in figure 6. A detailed description of each mode and the transitions between modes follows.

Figure 6. State diagram for control of LEDs on bracelet

Inactive sleep mode

On powerup the device will be in sleep mode, a press on the bracelet’s button will wake the device from sleep. Once the device is awake it will initially be in the controlled by buttons state.

Controlled by buttons

While in this state, the button can be pressed to cycle through some basic predefined LED patterns (solid colour, flashing etc.). A 3 second long press on the button will trigger the bracelet to start BLE advertising. If the button is pressed and held for 10 seconds, the device will turn off its LEDs, and return back to inactive sleep mode. While in the button control mode the SoC will also periodically search for messages from a central controller. If messages are being received over ANT that means the bracelet is at a live event and it will transition to being controlled by the central controller.

BLE advertising

While in the BLE advertising state the bracelets LEDs will flash blue to indicate that the device is attempting to connect to a smartphone. The bracelet can be paired from the smartphone app as described in the Smartphone Application section. As the bracelet does not have a display capable of showing digits efficiently, and the data being passed over the BLE link is not sensitive we will be using “Just Works” pairing which requires no passkey entry[14]. To mitigate the chance of connecting to the wrong bracelet in a concert setting, the BLE advertising name will be printed on the bracelet and there will be an option to pair using NFC instead. If the BLE advertising times out without pairing to a device, the SoC will transition back into the controlled by button mode, BLE advertising can be attempted again with another 3 second long button press.

Controlled by smartphone

Once paired to a smartphone over BLE, the bracelet's LED lights will be controlled solely by the connected smartphone. However, a long press on the button will forcibly disable the BLE connection and return LED control to the button. If the BLE connection is dropped and reconnection times out, the bracelet will go back into sleep mode. While connected over BLE and in smartphone control mode, the SoC will periodically search for messages from a central controller. If messages are received over ANT, it means the bracelet is at a live event and will be controlled by the central controller.

Controlled by central controller

When searching for a specific ANT device, all channel parameters including device ID, frequency, and channel period are preset and the device continuously listens on the specified frequency. Once a message from the correct ID is found, the ANT channel will synchronise to that message, scheduling its next receive window according to the pre-defined channel period and automatically adjust its timing based on the time of the next received packet. This is the extent of the ANT “connection”. Since the bracelets are acting as receivers only, an unlimited number of bracelets can theoretically track and listen in to the central controllers transmissions. Having bracelets as receive only enables us to have an asymmetrical setup where the central controller is transmitting at a much higher power than the bracelets and also saves us the trouble of managing individual connections with a large number of bracelets.

While receiving messages from the central controller over ANT, the SoC will be controlling its LEDs based on the data being received. If the device drops the ANT connection it will attempt to search and reconnect to the central controller for 5 minutes. If the ANT search times out then the bracelet will transition back into sleep mode.

Detailed Design

Bracelet SoC

The main Bracelet SoC must be capable of:

  • BLE connection to a smartphone
  • Receiving ANT packets from the central controller
  • Receiving an assigned group over NFC
  • Reading input from the physical button on the device
  • Reading accelerometer data from the onboard accelerometer via I2C
  • Driving the 3 onboard LEDs via I2C based on physical input, accelerometer data, and the wireless connections

When selecting a SoC there were multiple devices available that met some of the above requirements, however the best option was the nRF52832 by Nordic Semiconductor. The nRF52832 has a combined protocol stack for using BLE alongside ANT, it also has built-in NFC and supports I2C. The ultra low cost cousin of the nRF52832, the nRF52810, was also considered but its lack of NFC and I2C support made it not worth the cost savings.

A BT832 module by Fanstel which is a packaged nRF52832 with RF circuitry and PCB antenna will be used to limit the development cost of RF circuit design and wireless certification. This module is FCC certified and is a recommended third party module by Nordic Semiconductor. The nRF52832 SoC will be using the S312 SoftDevice, which is Nordic’s ANT and BLE combined protocol stack. The specifics of radio scheduling to manage running a BLE connection and ANT in coexistence is abstracted away by the SoftDevice API. The protocol stack is capable of up to four BLE connections in the peripheral role, and up to 15 individual ANT channels concurrently [11].

In order to monitor the onboard battery level, the battery will be connected to a voltage divider circuit that gets sampled by the nRF52832’s ADC. When the measured battery voltage passes below a certain level, the bracelet will go into sleep mode. On wake, the bracelet checks the battery voltage, if it is still below the required threshold it briefly indicates via the LEDs that it is out of battery and then returns to sleep mode.

In order to assign a group to a bracelet, a bracelet can be written to using NFC by the smartphone application. By using NFC scanning we can avoid having to pair over Bluetooth to assign a bracelet to a group. Using NFC enables the use case where event attendees have their bracelets scanned at the gate when entering a venue in order to assign a group based on the location of their seats. The nRF52832 has built-in support for NFC. While in sleep or button control mode the NFC reader is configured to interrupt and record the received signal. When a message is written over NFC, the bracelet will update its assigned group accordingly.        

Any of the three control modes can also set the bracelet into a mode that changes LED colours based on motion. To enable this we have an onboard accelerometer that interfaces with the nRF52832 over I2C. The main SoC will sample data from the accelerometer, and trigger lighting effects when a threshold acceleration value is reached.

The LED drivers are also connected and controlled via I2C, more details are provided in the LEDs and Drivers section.

Central Controller Transmitter

The purpose of the central controller transmitter is to forward messages from the desktop application to all the bracelets using ANT. The device receives messages from the desktop app over a USB connection. The device is also powered via the USB connection. The hardware of the central controller prototype consists of a nRF52832 SoC, along with an nRF21540 RF front-end module. The front end module is required so that we can achieve the range necessary for our application. All scheduling of lighting effects is being done on the desktop application itself, and the transmitter is only responsible for forwarding the data to the groups via 2.4GHz radio.

The transmitter will be using the ANT wireless protocol in a broadcast topology. ANT is an isochronous protocol, transmitting data at regular periods on a fixed frequency. ANT usage and configuration is channel-based, each ANT node can connect to other nodes via dedicated channels. These channels are automatically timeslotted by the ANT protocol stack. The configuration of a channel includes its type, the frequency that the channel will be transmitted at, the period in between messages, and some identifying data. For our application, the central controller will be using multiple master transmit-only channels, and each bracelet will have one slave receive-only channel.

Packet Format

As stated in Requirement 2.9, the central controller must be able to control up to 32 individual groups of bracelets at a refresh rate of 10Hz. In order to accomplish full colour effects, each packet contains a 16-bit colour value per group. The bracelet also needs to receive some control data with each packet which is used for transitions between colours, preset patterns, and enabling motion detection modes. The control data was originally estimated to be 16 bits, but further elaboration on the design determined that was overkill. By decreasing the allocated control data to 5 bits we are able to fit three groups in each standard 8 byte payload ANT packet. Each group needs a 21-bit message at a rate of at least 10Hz.

Figure 7. ANT data message

The ANT protocol stack on the nRF52832 supports up to 15 ANT channels at once. When a bracelet is assigned a group, it reads from a preset table to determine which ANT channel ID it will be listening for, as well as which group within a packet it is assigned to. With this format, a bracelet only has to track a single ANT channel.

To achieve the required refresh rate and latency the transmitter will be broadcasting at the maximum allowable rate. The maximum ANT channel rate for a single channel running on an nRF52832 is 200Hz [13]. We will need 11 total channels (32 groups/3 groups per channel) to accommodate all of our groups. This means that the frequency per channel is approximately 18Hz (200Hz/11 = ~18Hz), which gives us a considerable margin over the 10Hz required refresh rate. Therefore, the central controller will configure 11 ANT channels all with a rate of approximately 18Hz.

Operation

After being configured, an ANT channel will broadcast periodically and will schedule its timeslots between the other channels running on the device. Once that data for an ANT channel is set it will continue to broadcast that payload indefinitely until the data is updated. Our software on the nRF52832 will configure its 11 channels with the predefined channel IDs and then listen for serial messages from the desktop app. The messages from the desktop application will include the 21 bits of data in the same format as is sent to the bracelets and a group identifier. On reception of a message, the firmware running on the nRF52832 will find the correct channel and data offset for the group identifier provided and update its payload contents. Since our transmitter device is running off of USB power, we are fine with sending redundant messages and will continue transmitting the same data unless it is explicitly updated. Sending redundant messages will also serve to reduce the penalty of a missed packet. If a bracelet misses a packet and is still stuck in a previous state, it may be able to pick up the next packet and synchronise back with the rest of the bracelets within our 100ms latency requirement.

The scheduling of individual ANT channels is abstracted away by the SoftDevice interface, but channels will be scheduled in timeslots conceptually as follows.

Figure 8. ANT channel scheduling

Since only one channel can be transmitted at a time, this means that if the desktop application sends serial messages to update all groups at once, the actual messages sent to each bracelet will be offset from one another. Assuming there are 11 18Hz-channels, the most that one channel can be offset from another is 1s/18Hz = ~55ms. A 55ms offset is still within our 100ms latency requirement, and we do not expect it to be noticeable to the human eye.

The typical range of an ANT module (such as the nRF5282) is around 30 metres under ideal conditions, but can be increased through use of an external amplifier [13]. According to Nordic Semiconductor “the nRF21540 RF FEM’s +20 dBm TX output power and 13 dB RX gain ensure a superior link budget for between 16 and 20 dB improvement. This equates to a 6.3 to 10 times theoretical range improvement.” [10]. Considering ANT’s typical range of 30m with Nordic Semiconductor’s range extension estimation, the range of our ANT broadcasts should be extended to ~189-300m, well beyond our required distance.

LEDs and Drivers

The bracelet will contain three WS2812B LEDs. The WS2812B is a SMD 5050 LEDs with an integrated driver. The SMD 5050 package is a surface mounted, tri-colour LED with 5.0mm x 5.0mm dimensions. The mechanical dimensions can be seen in Figure 16. Note that all dimensions are in millimetres and the tolerance is ±01.mm unless otherwise noted.

Figure 16. WS2812B 5050 LED dimensions

The estimated power consumption for the WS2812B is a maximum of 60mA at 3.5V with the LED transmitting full brightness white. The part is capable of a lumen flux of 16-22 lumens. The WS2812B is capable of 256 brightness levels for each colour at a 30Hz refresh rate. The integrated driver in the WS2812B LED enables the main BLE SoC to use an I2C connection to control the lights. Using an LED with an integrated driver helps to reduce the development cost of designing drivers and enables us to control each LED individually.

The data to the WS2812B chips can be chained together in series and each LED can be individually addressed and controlled. This helps with simplifying the circuit needed in our bracelet, and expands our options for LED placement across the bracelet. A typical application circuit can be seen in Figure 17.

The popularity of these I2C controlled LEDs has resulted in abundant documentation and samples which will enable us to reduce the development time for this portion of the project. The tolerated input voltage for the WS2812B is stated by the manufacturer as being +3.5~+5.3V, although we found designs powering the LED off of as low as 3.3V without issue [15]. Bracelet Physical Inputs

The bracelet must be able to detect when a user is waving or clapping their hands (requirement 4.2). We will be using an accelerometer on the bracelet in order to detect movement. Our application does not require a high level of accuracy since we only need to detect large movements. We mainly looked for an inexpensive and low power option. For the bracelet’s accelerometer we have selected a MMA8653FC. The device takes 1.95 V to 3.6V as supply voltage and communicates through I2C to the SoC.

The bracelet also requires a physical button for input (requirement 4.1). The physical button will be located on the side of the bracelet housing. This button will be used to wake the device from sleep, and switch between modes as described in the Bracelet SoC section. The button will be connected to a GPIO pin on the main bracelet SoC.

Bracelet Power Supply

The bracelet will use a Lithium-Polymer (LiPo) battery to supply power to all of the onboard components. The dimensions of the LiPo battery are 30mm x 30mm x 8mm. A LiPo battery has a nominal voltage of 3.7V with a usable voltage range of 3.3V-4.2V. Since the bracelet is being worn on a wrist, the safety of using the LiPo battery is a major consideration. To mitigate the risks involved, the LiPo battery has an onboard overcharge, overdischarge, and short circuit protection and will be enclosed in a hard case.

The components that must be powered by the LiPo battery are:

  • nRF52832 Wireless SoC
  • 1.7-3.6V supply voltage
  • 3.3V logic level
  • MMA8653FC  Accelerometer
  • 1.95V-3.6V supply voltage
  • 3x WS2812B RGB LEDs
  • +3.5~+5.3V supply voltage

As the nRF52832 is an RF chip, it requires a relatively clean input voltage. To supply power to the nRF52832 and accelerometer, the LiPo battery will connect to a 3.3V low-dropout (LDO) regulator. For our bracelet we have selected an AP2112 based on a reference design for powering a nRF52 family chip with a LiPo battery [16]. A typical application circuit of the AP2112 is shown in Figure 18 where VOUT will be 3.3V.

Figure 18. Application Circuit for AP2112 Load Regulator

Although designs exist with the WS2812B LEDs being powered off of 3.3V, the manufacturer recommendation is a minimum supply voltage of 3.5V, therefore we cannot power the LEDs off of our 3.3V LDO, and will instead power them directly off of the LiPo battery.

The logical input voltage for the WS2812B LEDs is +/-0.5V of the VDD, which in our case is the voltage of the LiPo battery. Since the VDD will range from 4.2V to 3.3V, the logic level of 3.3V from the wireless SoC will be out of range for a portion of the battery’s cycle, and the I2C connection may falter. To ensure the wireless SoC can communicate with the LED drivers over I2C, a bi-directional logic level shifter circuit is required. For the logic level shifter circuit a BSS138 MOSFET, which is intended for this application, will be used.

In order for the bracelet to be considered rechargeable (Requirement 1.4), the product must provide a method of recharging the battery. We decided to use an onboard charging circuit that takes +5V input through a Micro-USB port. For the charging circuit we will be using an MCP73831 500mA IC. The MCP73831 can charge Li-Ion and Li-Po batteries and has thermal regulation to prevent overheating. This IC was chosen from the same reference design that we used to select the LDO [16]. A typical application circuit of the MCP73831 is shown in Figure 19 where VIN will connect to the Micro-USB interface.

In order to monitor the on-board battery level, the battery will be connected to a voltage divider circuit that gets sampled by the nRF52832’s ADC, as seen in the reference design [16]. The SoC will control the power supply to the accelerometer and LEDs, and prevent them from being powered when the device is in sleep mode.

Summary and Future Work

As a working prototype, this version of the product successfully meets the needs of the client at this stage. For strengths, the bracelet is battery powered and can act as a standalone device with or without a smartphone acting as a controller. Usage in a concert setting is also achieved and lighting effects can be created using the desktop application that was built specifically for this project.

A few weaknesses of the project involve the LEDs and battery that were selected, and minor circuit design choices that could be changed. Time was a large factor in decisions that were made in the design process, resulting in LEDs that were easy to work with but may be overpowered for this specific application. Batteries for the bracelet could potentially be designed in-house and may meet the requirements better than the one chosen for this prototype, but as time and cost were both factors, readily available off-the-shelf batteries were chosen. Having a group made up of students with little to no circuit design was a setback as minor changes could have been made to reduce the footprint of the PCB and better meet industry standards. As a prototype product, this weakness is not one of much concern for this project.

A main improvement that could be made to this project in future iterations would be reducing the size of the bracelet itself. As the current version is just the first prototype, the size is acceptable but to reach market quality a full production level design would need to be created. This was out of the scope of this project and as such was not a concern over the last eight months, but future versions would need more time spent on physical design.

Another addition that could be made to this project is centred around added functionality between the bracelet and the smartphone app. With this project being completed in prototype fashion, certain functionalities were cut out in order to meet the time constraints of the course. Future versions of this product could include phone notification functionality where the bracelet lights up when the user's smartphone receives a text message or other notification. This would increase the reusability of the product, giving it similar functionality to a smart watch.

Other functionality that the client had mentioned for future iterations included the capability to use the bracelet as a means of payment at concession stands around a venue. As the client, Koble, is working to develop an app that would integrate into current methods of obtaining concert tickets, they are also looking for ways to expand their company and adding payment methods to the bracelet would increase its feasibility as a reusable product. This is another feature that was determined to be out of scope for this specific product but is something that Koble will likely look into in the future.

Part 3: Risk Mitigation

The primary risk that we have identified for this project management period is the broad scope of the software features we plan to implement on our project. After meeting with our client, Jay, he brought this risk up and provided the guidance that he would rather see a subset of the features reliably than all of the features working unreliably. In order to mitigate this risk we have identified some key features as part of the minimum viable product for the prototype and have prioritized others lower (e.g. motion detection related features).

The integration of the accelerometer on our bracelet remains the largest unknown in our project. Time considerations and the limited number of accelerometers available within our budget prevented us from prototyping with the exact circuit and accelerometer IC that is present on our PCB. Unlike the rest of the bracelet circuit, we were unable to test on a breadboard prior to ordering the PCB, therefore we have comparatively less confidence and progress made on the firmware integration of the accelerometer into our project. This risk is mitigated by the fact that the accelerometer functionality is a lower priority feature for this iteration of the prototype. If we hit any major roadblocks, the accelerometer related features can be put aside without affecting the rest of our prototype.
